Personas and Storyboarding Case Study

Introduction

We've all been there: in a rush, with just a few items in hand, hoping for a quick exit, only to find ourselves trapped in a self-checkout maze. Error messages flash, the machine won't scan your coupon, and now there's a line forming behind you. What was supposed to save time suddenly becomes stressful.

This case study began with a simple question: how well do everyday users navigate CVS's self-checkout system? Through field observation, user interviews, and design tools like personas and storyboarding, I explored the gaps between what the interface assumes and what real people need. The goal wasn't just to critique, but to understand how interface design shapes the emotions and behavior of users in real-world moments.

Ultimately, this project reveals that even a familiar technology like self-checkout carries deep usability challenges, especially for users who are rushed, hesitant, or unfamiliar with the system. Their stories shaped my understanding of empathy-driven design and the power of observation in crafting better user experiences.

Part 1: Sketch & Interface Description

Sketch

Description of the Interface

The CVS self-checkout machine is designed to provide customers with a quick and independent way to purchase things without needing a cashier. Key components include a touchscreen interface for selecting options, a barcode scanner for scanning products, a card payment console, a cash input/output section, and a bagging area with a weight sensor. The interface aims to streamline the checkout process by reducing wait times and offering an alternative for users who prefer self-service.

Part 2: Observations & Interviews

Observations

Below are some key observations from watching customers interact with the CVS self-checkout machine:

  • The "bagging area" was often unclear to users, as bags were located opposite to where they were supposed to place their items. This resulted in slower checkout times, as the machine doesn't allow the next item to be scanned until it registers the previous item.
  • Many users struggled with scanning irregularly shaped items, requiring multiple attempts.
  • Payment processing caused delays, and users were often unsure when to begin using the seperate payment device.
  • Users also had trouble with inserting coupons into the designated slot, where they would often get lodged.
  • At least one user required employee assistance due to an error message that they could not resolve.
  • Experienced users completed the checkout process significantly faster than first-time users.

Interview Questions

The following questions were used to gather user insights regarding their experience with the self-checkout machine:

  1. Before today, how often have you used a self-checkout machine at CVS or elsewhere?
  2. What was the first thing you noticed when you approached the self-checkout machine?
  3. Can you describe your overall experience using the CVS self-checkout today?
  4. What, if anything, surprised you about the process?
  5. Were there any points in the process where you hesitated or felt unsure about what to do next?
  6. What aspects of the self-checkout process felt easiest to you?
  7. If you could change one thing about the self-checkout experience, what would it be?

Summary of Responses

Below are key insights from user interviews:

  • Most users expected a smooth and fast experience but were slowed down by minor technical issues.
  • Some users found the scanning process inconsistent and were frustrated by bagging area delays.
  • One user mentioned frustration over the coupon insertion slot.
  • Another user found the card payment process unintuitive, especially with tap-to-pay options.
  • Most users said they would use self-checkout again, but some said they would opt for a cashier if they had multiple coupons or items prone to scanning issues.

Part 3: Personas

Persona 1: Rushed Rachel

Description: Rachel is a busy mother of two with a full-time job who uses self-checkout to save time but often feels frustrated by delays in the process.

Interface Challenges:

  • Bagging area sensitivity: The system flags errors when she moves items too quickly.
  • Unclear coupon scanning process: She isn't sure when or where to scan coupons.
  • Error handling delays: Attendant assistance is required too frequently, slowing her down.

Why Rachel Represents Users: She's a common type of user at self-checkouts — busy, impatient, and task-focused. Many customers, particularly working parents or commuters, rely on self-checkout to speed up shopping but become frustrated when the system introduces unexpected delays.

Persona 2: Cautious Carl

Description: Carl is a 55-year-old retiree who is new to self-checkout and prefers traditional cashier interactions. He is careful and methodical but finds the interface confusing and intimidating.

Interface Challenges:

  • Overwhelming number of options on the screen makes it hard to focus on key tasks.
  • Unclear guidance on scanning and payment steps leads to hesitation.
  • Too many system alerts (e.g., “Please remove item”) cause confusion and uncertainty.

Why Carl Represents Users: He represents those who are hesitant or unfamiliar with self-checkout technology. Many older customers or those who don't use technology often face similar challenges. They need clearer step-by-step guidance and reassurance that they're doing things correctly.

Part 4: Storyboard

This storyboard follows Rushed Rachel, who wants to quickly purchase groceries before heading home to make dinner, but scanning issues, coupon confusion, and bagging system errors create frustration.

Lessons Learned

What started as a simple visit to a CVS self-checkout machine quickly became a window into the deeper frustrations of everyday users. I had never really stopped to consider how something designed for speed could so easily slow people down, especially under pressure.

By watching users interact with the machine and listening to their stories, I realized just how much the interface assumes familiarity and patience. A busy mom juggling errands, a retiree just trying to pay for batteries — both are vulnerable to design that doesn't guide or forgive. What struck me most was how small confusions (like where to scan a coupon or when to use the payment pad) added up to a frustrating experience that felt more like a test than a convenience.

Creating personas like Rachel and Carl helped me step outside my own assumptions. It wasn't just about observing pain points. It was about empathizing with urgency, uncertainty, and even embarrassment. Through storyboarding, I learned how to tell a user-centered story that makes abstract usability issues feel personal and real.

This project reminded me that good interface design isn't only about efficiency; it's about trust. When technology is confusing or rigid, people feel judged or excluded. Designing for those moments — when a user freezes, hesitates, or glances around for help — is where real accessibility begins.
